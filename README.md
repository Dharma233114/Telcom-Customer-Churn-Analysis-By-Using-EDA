This project analyzes customer churn in a telecom company to understand why customers leave the service. The main goal is to identify important factors that influence churn and suggest ways to improve customer retention.

The analysis shows that contract type plays a major role in churn. Customers with month-to-month contracts have a 42% churn rate, which is very high. In comparison, customers with one-year contracts have only 11% churn, and those with two-year contracts have just 3% churn. This clearly shows that longer contracts help in keeping customers for a longer time.

Payment method is another important factor. Customers who use electronic check have a 45% churn rate, which is the highest among all payment methods. Customers who use credit cards, bank transfers, or mailed checks have lower churn rates between 15–18%. This suggests that payment convenience and trust may affect customer decisions.

Customer tenure (how long they stay) also impacts churn. Nearly 50% of customers leave within the first year. Customers who stay between 1–3 years have around 35% churn, while customers staying more than 3 years have only 15% churn. This means the first year is the most critical period for customer retention.

The analysis also shows that 41% of senior citizens churn, compared to 26% of non-senior customers. Additionally, fiber optic users have a higher churn rate (30%) than DSL users (20%), possibly due to service expectations or pricing.

Overall, this project helps identify high-risk customer groups and provides insights to reduce churn. By promoting long-term contracts, improving payment methods, and focusing on early customer engagement, telecom companies can significantly improve customer retention and business growth.
